# Objection Handling — 12 Common Pushbacks

Every sales conversation has 1-3 objections. The goal isn't to "handle" them — it's to surface them early and make the buyer's concern visible. Below are the 12 most common, with responses that have actually closed deals.

## 1. "It's too expensive."

**Bad answer:** "We can come down a bit."
**Good answer:**
> "Most of the founders I work with say that on call 1 and don't say it on call 3. The ROI math: this engagement pays back if you add `[X]` customers / save `[Y]` hours. Want to walk through that with your numbers?"

If they still say no, **ask what 'expensive' is benchmarked to**:
> "Compared to what — your last agency, your first hire, or what you imagined this would cost?"

The benchmark answer tells you whether it's a price problem or a value-perception problem.

## 2. "Can you do it for `[X]` lakh less?"

**Don't reflexively discount.** Convert the ask into a trade:
> "I can hit that price by removing `[deliverable]`. Or I can hold the price and we get a longer commitment / a case-study credit / a faster decision. Which do you prefer?"

Discounting without trade signals weak pricing. Trading signals confidence.

## 3. "We're getting quotes from other agencies."

**Bad answer:** "We're better than them."
**Good answer:**
> "Smart. The right comparison isn't who's cheapest — it's who you'd actually want building this. Two suggestions: (a) ask each shortlist for one specific case where they shipped a similar problem in our timeline, and (b) ask who exactly will be doing the work. Most of the time, the cheap quote ends up being a junior team."

Don't trash competitors. Frame the comparison correctly. Buyers respect that.

## 4. "We don't have a designer / engineer to work with you."

**Good answer:**
> "That's actually our preferred setup. We're senior end-to-end — strategy, design, build, deploy. The reason most engagements need a client-side designer/engineer is to manage agency handoffs. We don't have those handoffs."

This becomes a *strength*, not a problem.

## 5. "Can you start tomorrow?"

If urgency is real and budget is approved → say yes, but with terms:
> "I can clear my Tuesday for kickoff. Two conditions: 50% advance hits this week, and you nominate one decision-maker for the duration. Both make me move fast without surprises later."

If urgency is a panic signal → slow them down:
> "I can, but my honest read is you'd get better work if we run a 90-min Mint workshop first. I'd rather start a week late and ship the right thing than start tomorrow and ship something we both want to redo."

## 6. "We need to think about it."

**Good answer:**
> "Of course. To make that thinking productive — what specifically would you want to know that you don't know yet? I'd rather answer it now than have you stuck on it for 2 weeks."

If they can name the gap, you can address it. If they can't, the "think about it" is usually polite no.

## 7. "Can you guarantee `[outcome / lead count / revenue lift]`?"

**Good answer:**
> "I can guarantee the deliverables and the quality bar. I can't guarantee revenue because that depends on your product, your sales motion, and the market. What I can do is share the case studies where comparable engagements moved comparable metrics — would that help?"

Anyone who guarantees outcomes is lying. Don't compete on lies.

## 8. "Why are you in Hyderabad? We're in `[Mumbai/Bengaluru/Dubai]`."

**Good answer:**
> "We host kickoffs in person in our HITEC City studio and run weekly demos on video. For engagements above ₹5 L, we travel to you on us. The Hyderabad base is actually why our pricing works — we keep overhead lean and pass the saving."

Turn perceived bug into actual feature.

## 9. "We had a bad experience with a previous agency."

**Good answer:**
> "Tell me what specifically went wrong. The pattern matters. If it was juniors doing senior work — that's our wedge. If it was scope creep — let me show you our SOW format. If it was timeline slippage — here's our weekly demo cadence."

Listen before responding. Most "bad experience" stories have a specific shape; address the actual pattern.

## 10. "Can we do this on a smaller pilot first?"

This is **good news**, not a stall. Embrace it:
> "Absolutely. Here's a 2-week pilot for `[smaller scope]` at `[scaled-down price]`. If we both like the working relationship, we roll into the full engagement at the originally quoted price minus the pilot fee."

Pilots that convert are 90%+. Pilots that don't usually reveal a real fit problem.

## 11. "Why isn't this on a retainer?"

**For project work:**
> "It can be — but I usually recommend ship-first, retainer-after. Here's why: a retainer that starts before there's something live is paying for hours, not outcomes. Once `[the site / the tool]` is live, I'd love to talk about a content + iteration retainer that actually compounds."

This delays the retainer conversation but elevates its value when you have it.

## 12. "Send me the deck."

**Good answer:**
> "We don't send a deck — we send tailored proposals after a Mint workshop. The deck is just the agency thinking, it's not the work. Happy to do the workshop free if you're decision-ready in 14 days, or run it as a paid ₹25K artefact. Either way, you walk away with something real."

Decks are commodities. Workshops are differentiators.

---

## Universal pattern

For every objection, the response template is:

1. **Acknowledge** ("Smart question" / "Most founders say that")
2. **Reframe** (turn the objection into the right question to be asking)
3. **Trade or filter** (what would I trade for what they need, or — if no trade exists — disqualify gracefully)

Never argue. Never beg. The goal is *clarity*, not victory. A clear no on call 1 is a better outcome than a soft yes that ghosts on day 30.
